Not long ago---by which I mean an annoyingly short time ago---I wrote a post
titled _I've bought a farm_. In it, I declared a hard reset: I was stepping away
from the metropolis, stepping away from the bureaucratic weight of my day job,
and, most emphatically, stepping away from the endless, abstract churn of
software. I wanted to trade the exhaustion of complex systems for the simple,
tangible reality of the soil.

In my head it sounded like this great "clean exit" and a graceful shutdown that
would allow me to find a moment of clarity, reflect on what the hell I've been
doing wrong all this time, and pick a path that sucks marginally less.

**It didn't work out**.

It turns out that when you try to completely abandon one way of being for
another, you don't actually escape the nature of systems. You just change the
interface. Almost immediately after arriving on the land, I found myself trapped
in a brutal, binary dilemma that stripped away the romanticism of "simple
living" and forced me to confront why I had burned out in the first place.

## The Binary Trap of the Land

When I decided to move to a farm, I frankly hadn't put much thought into the
actual process. The farm was there, bridges were there to burn, and I could just
pack my things and leave. Thus, without too much thought, I loaded my things in
the back of a car and just went to the farm. Frankly, I enjoyed the first few
nights, and in fact I will likely continue enjoying the fresh air and pretty
scenery, but I had not considered the fact that the reality of the environment
would force me into one of two extremes at the farm.

[relatively famous article]: https://notashelf.dev/posts/curse-of-knowing

My first option would be to fall into the exact same trap, one that I've decided
to name the "logistics trap". If you've seen my [relatively famous article]
where I discussed how I am prone to wanting to fix things upon seeing how
they're flawed and the curse _and_ the blessing that it is, you might be
catching on to exactly what has bothered me at the farm. To actively farm is to
plunge headfirst into a relentless operational matrix. Cultivating land,
sourcing equipment, managing soil chemistry, fixing failing irrigation---it
demands an obsessive, optimizing mindset. Within days I found myself sitting in
my kitchen---under cheap lighting, as my ears desperately clung to the sounds
and other stimuli outside---thinking that I wasn't escaping complex architecture
at all. I was just trading declarative syntax, academic rigor and a relatively
successful career for mechanical failures, supply chain logistics, and physical
exhaustion. I had turned the farm into another high-stress project to optimize,
applying the exact same cognitive pressure that drove me away from the city.

Then I considered the alternative: keep everything behind, but still step back.
This also meant that I would be keeping my hands clean and refusing to partake
in labor---simply _existing_ on the farm and treating it as a quiet retreat and
doing nothing. But land is not static, and living off-grid is not passive.
Sitting still meant watching my resources, my savings, and my personal runway
slowly and quietly deplete. In a somewhat ironic moment, I found myself thinking
about how it is the physical equivalent of a memory leak: a slow, stressful
drain with no sustainable future.

Ultimately, I was paralyzed between becoming a hyper-stressed agricultural
logistician or a passive observer watching the clock run out on my savings.

## The Illusion of the "Finished" State

Why did this hit me so hard? Because I had fallen into a classic cognitive trap:
**I had mistaken a change of scenery for a finished state.**

In software, we constantly battle the illusion that a project can eventually be
"done." We chase the perfect configuration, the clean architecture, the bug-free
release where we can finally step back and say, _there, it is finished._ But any
pragmatic developer knows this is a lie. **No software is ever truly finished.**
The moment you stop maintaining a codebase, bit rot sets in. Dependencies shift,
environments change, and the system begins to decay. Even if you somehow attain
the "perfect program" that never requires your attention after some point, you
realize that it _does_ still require your attention.

The land operates on the exact same principle, only with higher stakes. There is
no final, stable state on a farm. Nature is a system in constant, chaotic flux.
You cannot deploy a crop and walk away; you cannot freeze the state of a field.

I had tried to solve an internal exhaustion by migrating to an external
environment, hoping the land would provide the static peace I couldn't find in a
terminal or a metropolis. But the land doesn't offer static peace. It demands
continuous, never-ending refactoring.

> We don't burn out because we are building the wrong systems; we burn out when
> we expect those systems to eventually stop demanding our maintenance.

## The Compromise

You don't fix an architecture by burning the entire infrastructure to the
ground. You fix it through a clear separation of concerns. While I am a _little_
embarrassed to admit it, I realize now that I did not need a "total rewrite". It
was not a "just rewrite it in Rust, Rust = good" moment but a requirement for a
healthier boundary. So, I am making a compromise. One that respects both the
tangible world and the abstract one.

After only a week, I'm stepping back from the permanent off-grid experiment. I
need the stability and structure of my regular work, and I cannot sustainably
drain my resources in isolation. It led me to realize that the farm is a
sanctuary and not a vocation; I am keeping the farm, but I am changing its role.
It will be my weekend sanctuary---a place to physically disconnect from the city
and my job without the existential pressure of turning the land into a
self-sustaining operational logistics engine.

This also brings to you, my dear reader, the promise that I'll be back to
maintaining software. It's not like I stopped doing that anyway. Mostly, I
started abandoning old projects that I was maintaining for no good reason and
moved some more projects to my private Git server. To put it bluntly: I'm
continuing to write software. Not because I have to, and not as an all-consuming
escape from reality, but because the creative act of building minimalist,
functional tools is a fundamental part of how I think.

No software is ever truly finished. And as I sit here, bridging the gap between
the dirt of the countryside and the glowing rectangle of my screen, I've
realized I am not finished with it either.

See you next time, cheers.
